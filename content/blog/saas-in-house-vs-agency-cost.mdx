The in-house versus agency decision is rarely just about hourly rates. For a funded founder, the real question is whether the company needs to own a permanent product team today, or whether it needs a specialist build team that can turn a defined product into working software faster.

Both paths can be right. Both can also be expensive in ways that do not show up on the first spreadsheet.

## The Fully Loaded Cost of an In-House Team

A credible SaaS build usually needs more than one developer. Even a lean first version needs product thinking, interface design, frontend engineering, backend engineering, infrastructure, QA, and security judgment. One strong full-stack engineer can cover some of that surface area, but rarely all of it at production quality.

In the UAE, a senior product engineer can easily cost the equivalent of USD 85,000 to 140,000 per year before benefits, recruitment fees, visas, equipment, and management time. In Western Europe, senior engineers commonly land between USD 90,000 and 160,000 fully loaded, with higher costs in markets such as Germany, the Netherlands, Sweden, and the UK. In Australia, senior SaaS engineers often sit around USD 100,000 to 170,000 fully loaded once superannuation, hiring costs, tools, and overhead are included.

Now multiply that by a small team: one frontend engineer, one backend engineer, one product designer, one QA or automation engineer, and some DevOps or security support. Even if some roles are fractional, the annualized cost can move into the USD 350,000 to 700,000 range quickly.

That number does not include the cost of waiting.

## Recruiting Time Is a Product Cost

Hiring a good team takes time, especially if the company is early and cannot yet offer the stability, brand, or compensation of larger employers. A founder may spend two to four months sourcing, interviewing, negotiating, and onboarding. Senior candidates may decline late. Recruiters may charge 15% to 25% of first-year salary. New hires may need several weeks before they understand the product and can make architectural decisions confidently.

During that period, the product is not standing still. Competitors keep shipping. Investor timelines keep moving. Customer conversations keep creating expectations.

Recruiting time is not an HR cost. It is runway.

## Where Agencies Are Economically Strong

A specialist agency is usually strongest when the product scope is clear enough to build, but the company does not yet need a permanent internal department. The agency brings a team that has already worked together, already has delivery rituals, and already knows how to move from scope to design to implementation.

For a SaaS MVP or first production release, an agency engagement might cost less than the annualized cost of a full internal team while delivering within a tighter window. The founder avoids recruiting drag, employment commitments, and the management overhead of coordinating multiple new hires.

That does not mean agencies are automatically cheaper. A weak agency can burn money by building the wrong thing, hiding complexity, or leaving behind code the company cannot maintain. The economic advantage only exists when the agency is experienced in the product category and transparent about scope.

## Where In-House Wins

In-house teams become more compelling when the product is already validated, the roadmap is long, and the company needs constant iteration across many product surfaces. If the product is the company, eventually the company should own deep technical knowledge internally.

There are also strategic reasons to hire early. A technical co-founder, founding engineer, or senior product lead can protect the company from poor decisions, build institutional memory, and make future agency or contractor relationships more effective.

The mistake is assuming "in-house" means "cheaper." It usually means more control, more continuity, and more responsibility.

## A Practical Comparison

If you need a first release in three to five months, have a defined market thesis, and need experienced product delivery without building a department, an agency can be the more efficient route.

If you need years of product iteration, have enough capital to recruit carefully, and can manage technical talent well, an in-house team becomes a stronger investment.

Many funded startups use a hybrid path: hire one senior internal technical owner, then pair that person with a specialist agency for the first major build. The internal lead protects architecture and product knowledge. The agency supplies delivery capacity and execution speed.

## The Decision Founders Should Make

Do not compare an agency proposal to one developer's salary. Compare it to the actual team, time, risk, and management load required to reach the same outcome.

The right question is not "which option is cheaper?" The right question is "which option gets us to a reliable product with the least strategic waste?"

For some startups, that is a carefully hired internal team. For others, it is a specialist agency. For the best-run teams, it is often a deliberate sequence: agency for speed, internal leadership for continuity, and hiring once the product has earned the cost.
